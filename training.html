training

1. the approach

the approach focuses on speed, power, and consistency. training for the approach usually entails:

speed development days, which can consist of short repeated sprints (60m), short hurdle runs, sled pulls, olympic lifting, and more. the variety in exercise highlights the focus of not only speed, but also explosiveness, consistency, and endurance.

running is usually done with poles in hand in order to instill good approach mechanics and familiarize athletes with the feeling of running with a pole. pole vaulters emphasize tall posture, quick foot strikes, and consistent steps in order to maximize speed.

2. the takeoff

the takeoff focuses on speed, body position, and vertical height.

without a good takeoff, the rest of the jump is compromised. like the approach, the takeoff is practiced both on and off the runway in order to practice good technique without having the full impact of a jump.

the takeoff is practiced through shorter approaches on lighter poles, or through walking/sand drills. a good takeoff emphasizes a fully extended body position under the pole and a tall, explosive jump off the ground.

3. the swing

the swing, just as crucial as any other part of the jump, loads more energy into the pole and sets up the inversion.

the motion of the swing is one that is mimicked off-runway through various drills such as rope swings, gymnastics, high bar swings, and other unique devices. unlike gymnastics, pole vaulters are taught to keep their feet flexed instead of pointed.

the swing is taught majorly through a drive knee and a swing leg, which help create the angular momentum needed for a successful swing. some coaches, however, may teach the tuck-and-shoot swing instead, which is a bunching of the legs and body together before extending upwards. it is important for the body to be tight and unsegmented during the swing in order to maximize energy transfer. there is also contention between coaches who teach a neutral head position as opposed to a head-back position.

4. inversion & turn

the inversion and turn converts horizontal approach and swing energy into vertical lift and successful bar clearance.

the inversion is practiced through gymnastics-based drills and other calisthenics. athletes practice hanging from high bars, rings, and other apparatuses in order to build the strength and body awareness needed to invert properly. vaulters may also decide to practice from a shorter approach or on a lighter pole in order to work on inversion technique.

timing and patience are most emphasized here. even with a perfect inversion, without the right timing, it is easy for the vaulter to miss the turn and hit the bar too early or too late. coaches often use video analysis to break down the timing of the inversion and adjust a vaulter's technique or equipment accordingly. vaulters aim to be as tight and close to the pole during inversion in order to maximize vertical energy upwards.
